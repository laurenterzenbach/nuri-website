Research foundations

Nuri’s exercises borrow ideas from cognitive science and second-language research. These sources are starting points for reading — they are independent work, not endorsements, and they don’t map one-to-one to every screen in the app.

  • Spaced repetition (SRS) Spacing study episodes improves long-term retention; optimal gaps depend on how far out you will be tested. Cepeda, N. J., Pashler, H., Vul, E., Wixted, J. T., & Rohrer, D. (2006). Distributed practice in verbal recall tasks: A review and quantitative synthesis. Psychological Bulletin, 132(3), 354–380.
  • Mnemonics (keyword imagery) Linking a foreign word to a familiar sound-alike and a vivid image can substantially boost vocabulary recall in controlled experiments. Raugh, M. R., & Atkinson, R. C. (1975). A mnemonic method for learning a second-language vocabulary. Journal of Educational Psychology, 67(1), 1–16.
  • Interleaved practice Mixing problem types (instead of blocking one type at a time) often helps learners discriminate when to apply each skill — the idea behind varied “Daily Mix” style practice. Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007). The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning. Instructional Science, 35(6), 481–498.
  • Self-explanation prompts Explaining material to yourself while studying deepens understanding beyond re-reading or copying. Chi, M. T. H., Bassok, M., Lewis, M. H., Reimann, P., & Glaser, R. (1989). Self-explanations: How students study and use examples in learning to solve problems. Cognitive Science, 13(2), 145–182.
  • Adaptive difficulty Systems that select the next step based on what you already know (intelligent tutoring / step-based feedback) can approach the benefit of human tutoring in many studies. VanLehn, K. (2011). The relative effectiveness of human tutoring, intelligent tutoring systems, and other tutoring systems. Educational Psychologist, 46(4), 197–221.
  • Identifying errors (corrective feedback) Meta-analyses in SLA find reliable benefits from feedback that helps learners notice and repair their mistakes. Li, S. (2010). The effectiveness of corrective feedback in SLA: A meta-analysis. Language Learning, 60(2), 309–365.
  • Phonetic training (minimal pairs / perception) High-variability training on contrasting sounds (including minimal-pair style contrasts) can improve non-native perception and, with retention, production. Bradlow, A. R., Akahane-Yamada, R., Pisoni, D. B., & Tohkura, Y. (1999). Training Japanese listeners to identify English /r/ and /l/: Long-term retention of learning in perception and production. Perception & Psychophysics, 61(5), 977–985.
  • Shadowing Listening and repeating in near-real time builds listening, articulatory practice, and monitoring — common in interpreter training and L2 classrooms. Kadota, S. (2019). Shadowing as a Practice in Second Language Acquisition. Routledge.
  • Multimodal retrieval Combining words, images, and audio engages separate memory codes (dual coding); actively retrieving material beats restudying alone (retrieval practice). Clark, J. M., & Paivio, A. (1991). Dual coding theory and education. Educational Psychology Review, 3(3), 149–210. · Rowland, C. A. (2014). The effect of testing versus restudy on retention: A meta-analytic review of the testing effect. Psychological Bulletin, 140(6), 1432–1463.
